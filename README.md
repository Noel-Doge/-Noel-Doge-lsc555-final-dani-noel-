<h2>Introduction: The Mount Vernon Library </h2>

	 <img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/4/4d/Mount_Vernon_Ladies%27_Association_logo.png" style="width:300px;height:400px;">
	<p> The Mount Vernon Ladies Association (MVLA) is a non-profit organization that preserves, maintains, and runs what was George Wasghinton’s estate as a historical site. The association itself was founded in 1853 by Ann Pamela Cunningham, and since then has focused on the preservation and education about George Washington, his estate, and his legacy. While there were different, smaller library organizations within the organization of the decades, the MVLA would take a new approach with the construction of the Fred W. Smith National Library for the Study of George Washington building in 2013. It is a museum library designed to be used by MVLA staff, researchers, and by appointment only, not the general public.
    </p>
    <img src="https://i.imgur.com/5vqH72h.jpeg" style="width:500px;height:300px;">
    
<h2> Lesley Parilla and MVLA library </h2>
	<p> For this project, I reached out to the Chief Librarian of the Research Library, Lesley Parilla. She has worked with the MVLA at the George Washington Presidential Library for several years, and had plenty of experience to point me in the right direction. </p>
	
<h3>MVLA Library Mission</h3>
    <p> On the Library’s About The Library page, it states that, “Through its collections and programs, the George Washington Presidential Library at Mount Vernon supports scholarly research, fosters new scholarship, provides a platform for educational outreach, and serves as a center for leadership training, conferences, and gatherings inspired by Washington’s example.” </p>
<p>  <a href="https://www.mountvernon.org/library/about-the-library">(2: https://www.mountvernon.org/library/about-the-library)</a> </p>
    </p> More specifically, the Chief Librarian of the Research Library, Lesley Parilla, explains that the mission of the George Washington Presidential Library involves the effort to “document and interpret George Washington's life and legacy, the development of Mount Vernon as a historic site, and the MVLA’s role in its preservation. It also supports scholarship through online and in-person access to a repository of materials.” It also maintains an annual research Fellowship program. </p>
	&nbsp;
<h2>Background</h2>
 
<h3>MVLA library -- Fred W. Smith National Library for the Study of George Washington </h3>
<img src="https://i.imgur.com/rehRNrN.jpeg" style="width:500px;height:300px;">
<p>
	The George Washington Presidential Library at Mount Vernon preserves the historical records of the Mount Vernon Ladies' Association. The library is also home to the MVLA Archives, which preserves the records of the history of George Washington, of Mount Vernon, and of the MVLA itself. 
    </p>
	<p> As it says on the library’s about page: “The George Washington Presidential Library at Mount Vernon is a resource for scholars, students, and all those interested in George Washington, colonial America, and the Revolutionary and founding eras.”(1) While the rest of the estate is designed to be visited by thousands of guests every day as a historical site, the library is not the same. It is not open to the general public – except by appointment – and as such fulfils a very different role from a public library, or indeed, the rest of the museum site. </p>

	<p> <a href="https://www.mountvernon.org/library">(1: https://www.mountvernon.org/library)</a> </p>
	<p> Throughout the Mount Vernon Ladies’ Association’s time as the caretakers of Mount Vernon, there has been mentions of archiving and librarian work. While the earliest reference is from the 1885 Council Minutes, which simply talks about a Library existing, there does not appear to have been a separate building for a library. (2) In 1983, the MVLA finished constructing the Ann Pamela Cunningham Administration Building for both administration, and to have designated space for the library. Finally in 2013, the idea of an MVLA library finally came to fruition, and the construction of a new research library was finished. The Fred W. Smith National Library for the Study of George Washington was designed to expand the library’s collections, have a greater capacity for those collections, as well as support the ongoing education and preservation work for the estate. </p>
	
	<p>  <a href="https://www.mountvernon.org/library/about-the-library">(2: https://www.mountvernon.org/library/about-the-library)</a> </p>
	
	<p> The George Washington Presidential Library’s General Collections contains more than 19,000 print and electronic holdings. Its Special Collections contains approximately 2,800 rare books and a large collection of manuscripts that document the lives of George Washington’s family, the Custis family, and the history of Mount Vernon before 1853. It also possesses a rare book collection that includes 105 volumes from George Washington’s personal library and a collection of maps from the 18th century. </p>
	
	
<h2>Evaluation</h2>
    <p>
The George Washington Presidential Library has a robust website with all of the expected search options that an online library or museum collection would have. It has only a moderate level of metadata for many of its 18th-19th century documents, but is still accurate and effective for someone with LIS experience. Due to the very lengthy titling of some objects, it could be particularly difficult to find some of the documents for the layperson. Much of Mount Vernon is designed for a wide general public to access, but the library is not set up like a public library would be, as it is a research library. An LIS professional or researcher would not have any trouble with interacting with, and using, the system. The friction between this library designed for internal MVLA use, researchers, historians, and LIS professionals being connected to a public historical site open to any and all will be the primary focus of this project.    </p>
    <p> 
        We also looked at the wider workflow involved with this information, specifically a rather frustrating process in which Abigail communicates in writing with patrons requesting access to specific documents or collections. Based on our observations, we have chosen to focus on simplifying the input system and patron request process, and clarifying some of the confusing prompts within the system.
    </p>


<h2>Challenges</h2>

<h3> Problems Outlined by the Chief Librarian of the Research Library </h3>

<p> The primary issues that will be explained, with potential solutions offered, will be those given by Lesley Parilla. As she mentions,</p>
<p> ”One recurring challenge for museum libraries is that visitors often view the library as an extension of the museum and expect to experience it in the same way they would the estate. In reality, we function primarily as a research library and are not staffed to provide that type of visitor experience. Our primary patrons are staff, volunteers, fellows working on estate projects, and independent researchers. This mismatch in expectations can create friction with the public, particularly around access and understanding the library’s core purpose.” </p>

<p> She additionally mentions that the Rare Book Room at the Library of Congress faced a similar challenge, also suffering from increased visitor demand. This puts a strain on the staff as, while they can process research appointment and reference questions, they do not have the staff or capacity to do public library work as they are a private, research library for the museum.  </p> 


<h2>Conclusion</h2>
	<img src="https://i.imgur.com/4rIviDf.jpeg" style="width:500px;height:300px;">

    <p> The Mount Vernon Ladies’ Association runs a large historic site designed to preserve a large estate that was owned by George Washington, and educates thousands of the general public every day. The MVLA’s George Washington Presidential Library is not designed to be a part of that public education, but it is instead a critical part of the historical preservation and non-public facing work that happens on the estate every day. Many guests are surprised by this, and their requests distract and hinder the staff from properly assisting the MVLA staff, LIS professionals, and researchers that the library is designed to assist. Overall, the system as-is works perfectly well for MVLA staff and researchers that need it for their work, and the LIS issues the system faces do not come from the system itself. Some solutions to these issues, either lightweight ones meant to signal the general public away from the library, and potentially to other resources, can help alleviate this pressure. Other solutions, like ones that would require much more investment, would also fix this issue, but might be far more costly in manpower time or cost. 
    </p>
